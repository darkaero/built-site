April 2025 Recap

In April, the first phase of wing attach lug testing was conducted to simulate a range of positive G load conditions. Another contract customer airframe was shipped, while several others continued to be developed in parallel.

Airframe Testing
Construction of the wing attach lug test rig was completed, and it was configured on the airframe for positive G load tests. The positive G configuration consists of a long aluminum frame interfaced with the top of the fuselage and a second smaller aluminum frame bolted into the wing attach lugs. Turnbuckles running between the two frames pull them together to generate reaction loads in the wing attach lugs and simulate wing lift loads. 

Additionally, the test rig allows the position of the turnbuckles to be adjusted forward and aft to simulate varying aerodynamic pitching moments produced by the wing at different airspeeds and control surface deflections. With two independent turnbuckles, asymmetric loads can also be generated. This enables tests to be conducted that simulate conditions that apply more load to the lugs on one side of the airframe compared to the other such as spins and “Rolling G’s”. Although pilots generally do not intentionally perform drastic pitch and roll acceleration at the same time in normal category aircraft, it can sometimes occur in emergency situations. 

To simplify the setup for initial testing, lateral angular deflection of the lugs due to wing deflection was not included in the test. Testing began at lower loads to verify the rig and test setup and incrementally progressed to a margin beyond limit loads. 

Additional rigging and equipment was set up to impart aft-directed loads on the lugs to simulate drag loads experienced by the wing. Drag loads and lift loads were applied to the lugs at the same time during one of the positive G load tests. This testing served as an initial benchmark for the fuselage structures as well as for the test stand itself to confirm the airframe and rig could react the test loads without damage.

The testing showed positive results and gave confidence to proceed with the remaining test conditions at different wing lug deflection angles driven by elastic wing root rib angular deflection. These angles are important to simulate as they more accurately account for the flex the wings will experience under load and change the angle of the load applied to the lugs. This will assess the lugs not just for their shear strength but also their peel strength in response to wing flex. The remaining angles at positive G will be simulated next. Following this, the test will be reconfigured to simulate negative G loading conditions, which will pull down on the lugs instead of up as seen in the positive G test.
