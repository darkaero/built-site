Engine Mount and Firewall Load Testing
With the vertical stabilizer and empennage structures validated under lateral loading conditions, the DarkAero 1 prototype structural ground test campaign transitioned to engine mount and firewall static load testing. In January, the engine mount structural design was re-reviewed, the respective load test rig was designed, and initial ‘bench tests’ were performed to begin proving out the test setup. 
Ryan Stube and Keegan Karl install the engine mass simulator in preparation for engine mount testing.

As a review, the engine mount is a metallic structure that transfers all engine and nose landing gear loads into the rest of the airframe. The current engine mount design is bolted to the forward face of the composite firewall with four bolts. Internal longitudinal bulkheads are positioned in the fuselage near these mounting bolts to assist the firewall in carrying engine loads. The firewall and internal bulkheads of the fuselage are manufactured from honeycomb sandwich panel. Hardpoints were bonded into the honeycomb structure during fuselage assembly to react compression loads without locally crushing the honeycomb panel firewall. The DarkAero 1 prototype engine mount is designed for manufacturability. Though the team does have welding capabilities, the current engine mount design does not require any welding and therefore eliminates potential weld specific failure modes and assembly fixtures for the prototype aircraft.

To balance program efficiency while still maintaining a rigorous safety-first ground test stance, the engine mount and firewall will be tested at the same time. Typical engine mount load tests can be performed on a test rig that is separate from the aircraft by leveraging existing characterized airframe strength values. Being a new design, the firewall and forward fuselage structure of the DarkAero 1 prototype need to be tested in addition to the engine mount to prove the composite airframe can support the loads imparted by the engine mount.

Engine loads can be primarily broken down into four different sources: thrust, torque, inertial, and gyroscopic loads. Thrust and torque are dictated by the engine-propeller performance and atmospheric conditions. Flight altitude and air temperature change the available oxygen content for the engine and propeller to convert into torque and thrust. Specific to the engine mount and airframe structures, the engine thrust line placement dictates how thrust loads are dispersed into the supporting structure. Engine manufacturers provide a mean torque value at a specific RPM, however this is not the peak torque load value imparted onto the engine mount and airframe during operation. Piston engines produce a torque pulse that exceeds the mean torque value every time a piston reaches its power stroke. Increasing the number of pistons in an engine results in less severe torque pulses as more power strokes occur within a single crankshaft rotation. Most aircraft engine installations utilize rubber mounts that can reduce the high-frequency torque pulses transmitted to the engine mount by as much as 80-90% in correct conditions.

Inertial loading is dictated by the acceleration of the aircraft while in flight. During steady, level flight, the engine imparts a 1G load on the engine mount, equal to its static weight. When maneuvering the aircraft, acceleration in the vertical and/or lateral axes produces higher loading on the engine mount. In the same way that a fighter pilot’s head can feel like it weighs nearly 100 lbs while pulling Gs, the engine can ‘feel’ significantly heavier on the engine mount when maneuvering the aircraft. The engine mount is designed to handle normal category operating loads with a safety factor. This structure is one of the reasons why an aircraft’s maneuvering speed reduces as gross weight reduces. Lightening an aircraft by removing fuel or payload and then pulling 3.8Gs will stress the wing less as less lift needs to be produced to reach the same load factor. This maneuver will still stress the engine mount the same amount however regardless of the current total gross weight as the engine does not get any lighter. In uncoordinated turns or spins, lateral inertial loading can also occur. In addition to inertial loading, up, down, left, or right, pitching or yawing the aircraft introduces gyroscopic moments. Gyroscopic precession of the propeller and engine rotating assembly experiencing torque loads means these moments act 90 degrees to the angular motion of the aircraft nose.

In addition to supporting the engine, the DarkAero 1 engine mount acts as the nose gear attachment point as well as an extension and retraction mechanism housing. This introduces landing loads into the engine mount assembly during landings and ground operations. These loading conditions were replicated to a certain degree during landing gear drop testing.
Ryan Stube and Keegan Karl preparing for the first load tests on the engine mount.

The majority of prototype structural load testing to date has used the engine mount and engine mass simulator as a counterbalance or reaction load application point on the aircraft. In doing so, the engine mount has experienced varying degrees of static and dynamic loading conditions that help provide assurance in the design going into a dedicated engine mount load test campaign. In January, the engine mount load test was designed and set up to allow all four primary loading conditions to be applied to the engine mount simultaneously. Initial tests were performed to prove out test stand operation and load application techniques, make sure loads can be reliably applied, and verify structural deflection measurements can be safely recorded.

Initial load testing showed additional focus needs to be placed on the commercial off-the-shelf rubber engine mounts that are installed between the engine and the engine mount. Discussions with the engine manufacturer have been occurring to learn more about the rubber engine mount properties and ensure testing is occurring with the most up-to-date hardware. Further understanding of the rubber engine mount load capabilities will help ensure the aircraft will not incur unintentional damage under load allowing engine mount testing to continue into February. Once the firewall and forward fuselage composite structure has been validated to known loading conditions for flight tests, future engine mount testing could occur off of the aircraft in a dedicated test cell.
