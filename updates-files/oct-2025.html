October 2025 Recap

Progress on the DarkAero 1 continued in October, with efforts focused on structurally testing the wing-to-fuselage interface. Another session of the DarkAero Aerospace Composites Course was held, additional manufacturing equipment was installed, and another contract customer airframe was completed. 
Ryan and Keegan apply simulated flight loads to the wing-to-fuselage interface.

Airframe Testing
The wing-to-fuselage interface was structurally tested in October, building upon previous airframe structural validation work. This interface consists of four carbon fiber lugs extending below the cockpit armrests that transfer lift loads from the wing into the fuselage. Each lug is machined from solid carbon fiber flat stock and secondary bonded directly to the side skins of the fuselage with no additional mechanical fasteners. Additional wet layup carbon fiber tapes are added to the edges of the lugs to help further distribute loads and reduce stress concentrations in the bondline of the joint. Four wing mounting bolts are installed laterally through the lugs and anchor into the wing root ribs. The geometry of the wing mounting lugs inherently allows them to experience angular deflection matching the deflection in the wing root ribs as the wing is subjected to lift loads. This behavior was characterized during the recent wing load testing to inform the setup for fuselage lug tests that replicate in-flight loading conditions.
Exagerated simulation of negative g loads being applied to the wing to show how the root ribs deflect at an angle relative to the wing mount lugs on the fuselage.

Angular deflection data from the wing tests guided the design of the wing lug test fixture. CNC-machined components were built to interface with the fuselage lugs and reproduce the measured wing root rib geometry. To enable proof load testing in both positive and negative lift directions, multiple sets of simulator parts were machined with the corresponding root rib deflection angles. During installation, these simulator components deflected the lugs into the same flexed positions they experience when the wing bends under load.
The fuselage configured for negative g load tests on the wing mount lugs.

Wing mount lug load testing then continued with the newly machined test stand components installed. The chordwise lift distribution on the wing varies based on angle of attack, airspeed, and control surface deflection, which causes the loads on each of the four wing attachment lugs to not be equal. This means the load application point could not be positioned equidistant from each lift lug. Calculations were previously performed to determine the specific location of each load application point with respect to the wing mount lugs to accurately represent flight lift load distribution. Due to the dynamic nature of an aircraft operating envelope, several different load cases were tested in both the positive and negative lift load directions. 
Ratchet straps were used during load tests of the wing mount lugs to apply simulated drag loads.

The load application positions were updated prior to each test to place more load on the forward, aft, left, or right lugs. The structure as a whole was taken to both positive and negative proof load values multiple times throughout the test campaign. Some of the tests stressed the forward lugs to their respective individual proof load value while other tests stressed the aft lugs to their respective individual proof load value. The elastic compression or elongation of the wing mount lugs with respect to the local fuselage sideskin area was measured at peak load to quantitatively define how the structure reacts under load. 

Using turnbuckles to generate loads instead of sandbags allowed for a safe, tightly controlled testing environment given the small working area in the fuselage. With only two turnbuckles producing the same force as all the sandbags used for wing proof load testing, it was deceivingly easy to simulate the aircraft operating loads. At times, the airframe was supporting the equivalent weight of multiple g's, yet the structure showed no visible indication of the forces involved apart from the readings on the load cells. 

The wing mounting structure passed proof load testing with no negative findings, and it is now accepted for flight. This was a major proof load test for the fuselage as it not only significantly loaded the wing mount lugs, but also induced shear loads in the fuselage side skins between the lugs, and reacted the imparted loads against the forward and aft regions of the fuselage. Passing this test is an exciting milestone to have completed and it is now checked off the Milestones section of the website.

Manufacturing
Manufacturing capability was increased in early October, with the installation and commissioning of an Autometrix Carbon automated cutting machine. This machine is capable of accurately cutting intricate shapes from prepreg and dry fabric. While assembly, setup, and calibration took nearly a full week, this equipment addition will significantly accelerate the preparation of composite ply kits in scaled manufacturing of composites structures. Instead of relying on manual cutting, the CNC cutter can produce precise, repeatable patterns at a much faster pace. It will also reduce variability, increase accuracy, and free up time to focus on more challenging manufacturing processes. The cloth cutting machine was immediately put to work cutting prepreg carbon fiber for an R&D project, and it will be used to accelerate other manufacturing projects going forward.

DarkAero Courses
Session 26 of the DarkAero Aerospace Composites Course was held in October, with students attending to gain composites experience for applications in kitplanes, eVTOL aircraft, and robots. Hands-on demos allowed students to work with carbon fiber, glass fiber, and aramid fiber fabrics. Wet layup, vacuum bagging, infusion, and prepreg processes were demonstrated to highlight the range of material variables that are impacted by manufacturing methods. Samples built in class were destructively tested to demonstrate the different strengths and stiffnesses that are achievable with different fiber types and layup schedules. The next session of the course has not been scheduled yet, but the online version of the course remains available for anyone seeking immediate access to the course material.
