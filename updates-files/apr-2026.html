April 2026 Recap

Progress continued on the DarkAero 1 prototype in April with further review and development of the aileron control system. More composite assemblies shipped for contract customers, new equipment was added to the shop, and interviewing ramped up to grow the team.

Control System
With the conclusion of the majority of primary load structure testing in March, focus shifted to a more thorough review of the aircraft's control system. This review uncovered deficiencies in the original design of the aileron control system. Although homebuilt experimental control systems can be mechanically simple, there are many requirements that need to be met for a system to be airworthy and safe. Mass distribution in the control surfaces, aerodynamic pressure distribution about the control surface pivot axis, mechanical advantage of the system, stiffness of the components under load, and free play of the total assembly all affect how an aircraft handles in the air. Adequate control of each axis (pitch, roll, and yaw) prior to first flight is a non-negotiable requirement for safety. Redesign is underway for the aileron control system to reach a flightworthy state for the prototype aircraft with the least amount of rework necessary.

DarkAero Capabilities
While development of the DarkAero 1 prototype has followed a non-linear path, underlying manufacturing capabilities at DarkAero have continued to advance steadily and deliberately. This progress includes growth of the team, expansion of manufacturing space, the addition of new equipment, development of quality systems, and continued manufacturing of complex hardware programs for external customers.

Much of this work has taken place behind the scenes under NDAs. However, these programs have provided valuable opportunities to build and refine the processes required to produce high-quality composite structures in a repeatable and scalable way. The experience gained through designing, building, and delivering flight hardware has directly contributed to improvements in tooling strategies, manufacturing workflows, inspection methods, and overall production readiness.

In the early stages of the DarkAero 1 prototype, scaling beyond a first article represented a significant unknown. As capabilities have matured, that challenge has become increasingly well-defined. Questions around how large composite structures would be handled, packaged, and shipped were open challenges. Today, building and shipping structures the size of wings and fuselages has become a routine part of operations. The path to kit production now resembles a series of approachable tasks extending established capability to DarkAero 1 kit manufacturing.

Although much of this progress is not always visible in prototype updates, it represents an important component of overall program momentum. The foundation required to support DarkAero 1 kit production continues to take shape alongside ongoing development work.

When learning about composites, attention is often drawn to raw materials such as carbon fiber and epoxy resin. However, the molds and tooling required to produce high-quality composite components are just as critical and are often underappreciated. Continued interest from students led to the development of a dedicated course focused on this aspect of the process.

Previously referred to as the Mold Making Course, this offering has recently been expanded and renamed the DarkAero Aerospace Mold and Tooling Course. The updated curriculum now includes not only mold fabrication, but also assembly fixtures, drill jigs, trim tools, inspection gauges, and workholding strategies developed through the production of precision composite hardware for demanding aerospace applications. As projects scale from individual parts to full assemblies, these tooling considerations become increasingly important, and the course content has evolved to address that progression.

DarkAero and UW Madison
In April, DarkAero was selected to join an industry advisory board for the University of Wisconsin–Madison College of Engineering, with the first meeting held at the end of the month. The board consists of alumni and industry representatives who work with UW-Madison to help align the engineering curriculum with current industry needs.

This involvement builds on an ongoing relationship with the university, where the DarkAero founding team earned their engineering degrees. Over time, that connection has included technical collaboration such as ground vibration testing for the DarkAero 1 and composite material validation work through the Polymer Engineering Center.

This engagement provides an opportunity to contribute real-world engineering and manufacturing perspective to the curriculum while maintaining close ties to academic research and the next generation of engineers.
