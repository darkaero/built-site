February 2026 Recap

DarkAero 1 prototype engine mount and firewall structural load testing continued in February with a focus on verifying structural integrity under combined loading conditions. Loads simulating a maximum positive G gust encounter resulted in further inspections and modification to the firewall structure prior to additional testing. A new manufacturing technician joined the team, and candidate interviews continued in the search to hire additional technicians, engineers, and operations team members. 

Engine Mount and Firewall Load Testing
Aerospace structures are tested on the ground before flight testing to find any weaknesses that need to be addressed prior to them potentially becoming safety issues in flight when lives are at risk and it is not easy to simply remove the load or safely land the aircraft. The engine mount and firewall load testing performed on the DarkAero 1 prototype accomplished this goal and built confidence in these structures.

Engine mount and firewall structural testing continued in February by performing multiple individual load test cases to respective proof load values. This means only the thrust proof load was applied, or only the torque proof load was applied, etc. The engine mount and firewall structure handled these individual proof load conditions without any findings or indications of significant strain. 

In reality, different engine load components are acting on the aircraft at the same time. For example, the propeller is producing thrust while the engine is also producing torque. With this in mind, the test campaign proceeded into combined loading conditions that more accurately simulated different phases of the aircraft flight envelope. Technically a single, aggressive test case could be performed where all four primary loads (thrust, torque, inertial, and gyroscopic) are simultaneously applied at their maximum possible magnitude, but doing so would inaccurately over-test the structure. Each of the four primary engine loading conditions have several variables that factor into their magnitude throughout the entire aircraft flight envelope. The combined ground loading test cases therefore reflect real world operating conditions with a safety factor applied to ensure the proof load values bound limit loads. 

Combined load testing started with simulating takeoff conditions. After passing this test case, additional conditions were simulated. Within 6% of the target loading conditions of a high speed, positive G, pitch up, gust encounter test case, a subtle acoustic event occurred localized to one of the upper engine mount attachment points within the firewall. Testing of novel composite structures is often performed in a quiet environment to allow test engineers to record acoustic data potentially produced by the structure while under load, which may be indicative of material strain relief or cracking. The test stand load application methods allowed for this initial event to be further studied in a controlled environment with low risk of a cascading structural failure. While not desirable, this type of event highlights the importance of carefully considering all failure modes of both the test article and the test stand itself when designing and planning a load test. For structural ground testing, directly applying loads to the aircraft is oftentimes the easy part. The challenge of this testing centers around applying the load in a controlled manner that allows energy to be safely released while preventing unnecessary further damage to the airframe in the event of a failure or even suspected failure.  

Post test investigation showed an adhesive crack initiation point located between the firewall bulkhead and one of the longitudinal bulkheads behind the firewall. The findings indicated that localized stress concentrations exceeded the capabilities of the firewall-to-bulkhead joint. The probable cause of the disbond was traced to a design oversight regarding the load dispersion between the primary support element and a neighboring bulkhead joint while under combined loading conditions. While pretest analysis did not explicitly predict this failure at the applied load, this failure scenario was recognized as one of the potential outcomes of the static load test and is exactly the reason why aerospace structures are tested in a controlled environment on the ground and not in flight. After detailed visual inspection was performed and the root cause was identified, the team quickly transitioned to redesigning and repairing the firewall support structure. 
Revised engine mount support structure compared against the CAD design.

Since the time when the original firewall support structure was manufactured and assembled, DarkAero manufacturing capabilities have grown and allowed for more advanced part geometries to be manufactured in a shorter timeframe. The new design revision leverages advancements in prepreg manufacturing and 3D printed molding techniques to address the experienced failure mode, as well as other potential failures at even higher loading conditions. New parts were designed and reviewed, molds were 3D printed and prepared, and parts were laid up and cured in a little over a week. 
Revised engine mount support structure.

The new parts are not an exact production intent iteration as this modification also had to consider aspects of the existing structural repair. Even with the additional repair requirements, the test data obtained and resulting modified prototype structure will help inform the DarkAero 1 production kit design in the future. Through the second half of the month, the new parts were installed and a localized post cure was completed.
Ryan Stube adjusting airframe counterweights for the next test. 

After modification work was finished, the aircraft was placed back in the test stand towards the end of the month. The same vertical gust encounter combined loading conditions were applied to the new structure with positive results. The revised structure also passed negative g-load conditions as well as simulated takeoff loading conditions where thrust is the highest. The airframe passing these proof load test cases paves the way for further validation of the aircraft operating envelope. It is always rewarding to see quick improvements directly correlated with lessons learned.
Keegan Karl and Ryan Stube setting up the test rig following installment of the revised engine mount structure.

Courses
As the capabilities of DarkAero have expanded and matured, the curriculum of the DarkAero Aerospace Composites Course has been updated based on real world design, manufacturing, and testing of composite structures. Most recently, a new module on high temperature capable BMI resin systems has been added to the course curriculum. This material is typically only used in the most demanding applications such as engine nacelles, radomes, and launch vehicles. With the March 12-13 session of the course sold out, the next available session is scheduled for June 25-26. The course material has been continuously refined over the past four years, and covers both the theoretical and hands-on aspects of aerospace composite materials and manufacturing. Live demos of wet layup, vacuum bagging, infusion, and prepreg allow students to gain experience in a broad range of manufacturing processes so the right process can be selected for their application. Fiber reinforcements including carbon, glass, aramid, and boron fibers are discussed, as well as approachable methods for selecting fabric weave types, resin systems, core materials, adhesives, and equipment.
