November 2025 Recap

November progress on the DarkAero 1 prototype was focused on final review and validation of the empennage lifting surfaces and modifications to the control system to reduce system compliance. A new manufacturing technician joined the team, and candidate interviews continued in the search to hire additional technicians and engineers. 
DarkAero co-founder, Keegan Karl, inspecting the DarkAero 1 prototype while configuring hardware for vertical stabilizer load tests.

Empennage Testing
With the wing structure validated to handle broader edge-case operating conditions, attention shifted to a detailed review of the horizontal stabilizer flight loads. The review was conducted to confirm that the original proof-load tests still provided sufficient margin across the full range of longitudinal stabilizing, maneuvering, and gust loads. The aircraft longitudinal stability derivatives were also reviewed to ensure adequate elevator power will be maintained across the intended C.G. envelope. The original calculation methods used to design the DarkAero 1 were compared against other theories and methods to further verify adequate stability and loads. 

This review focused primarily on stick-fixed power-off and power-on static stability, and future control system test reviews will cover stick-free stability calculations. Calculations and secondary review showed the horizontal stabilizer structure remains structurally sound for flight testing with a sufficient positive margin as verified by previous static ground proof load testing. This includes the empennage structure when subjected to the full envelope of longitudinal, pitching loads.
Load tests were previously performed on the horizontal stabilizer (above). A secondary review conducted in November determined additional horizontal testing would not be required after increasing the wing load envelope.

After the horizontal stabilizer review was completed, analysis turned to the vertical stabilizer which involves a separate set of challenges. The horizontal stabilizer is closely connected to the wing load as a change in load on the horizontal stabilizer produces a change in load on the wing and vice versa. Although aircraft yaw and roll are coupled, the vertical stabilizer loads are more separated from the rest of the aircraft. This essentially leads to a more localized analysis on the vertical stabilizer at maneuvering speed. In addition to review of loads, the static directional stability of the aircraft was verified with separate methods and theories than used for original DarkAero 1 design efforts.
DarkAero Engineer, Ryan Stube, reviewing vertical stabilizer loads.

A pre-test review was held towards the end of the month and efforts to set up the vertical stabilizer static ground proof load test began. Previous vertical stabilizer load testing was conducted that focused on the vertical stabilizer and its bonded connection to the fuselage. The goal of the upcoming test will be to not only verify the vertical stabilizer structural integrity in the lateral direction, but the empennage lateral structural integrity as well.
A steel version of the rudder pedal system (left) will be tested against the existing carbon composite version (right) to investigate compliance improvements for the control system.

Control System Modifications
After empennage lateral proof load testing is complete, control system load testing will resume. Previous control system testing showed that the first iteration of the control system was not flight capable and requires increased stiffness. Initial fabrication steps of a steel version of the rudder pedal assembly have been completed. Parts were dry fit together, cleaned, prepped for welding, and assembly jigs were manufactured.

The aircraft control system represents an interesting manufacturing case study where equally compelling arguments can be made for the components being made out of composites or metal alloys. The dramatically different manufacturing processes and mechanical properties between metal and composites yield different results that will be exciting to quantify through further control system testing. Ultimately, the team remains flexible with manufacturing and design techniques and will be pursuing the design that best meets the requirements. 
DarkAero Engineer, Gage Kuszmaul, 3D scanning a CNC machined landing gear component.

Manufacturing
Capabilities at DarkAero have continuously advanced to enable DarkAero 1 kit manufacturing at scale and better serve customers on contract projects. Engineering and manufacturing of composite structures has always been showcased, and recent updates have featured automation, testing, and additive manufacturing. Another capability at DarkAero that has not been highlighted previously is precision measurement, made possible through the use of a FaroArm CMM equipped with both probing and 3D scanning systems.

The FARO setup provides a way to accurately capture dimensions and geometry of complex 3D surfaces ranging from small hardware components up to composite structures as large as an entire airframe. The system has mainly been used to compare CNC machined parts and tooling against the intended geometry defined by CAD models. Airfoils and compound curves in cowlings and fairings are difficult to measure accurately through other methods, while the FaroArm and scanner are well suited for this. The scanner can capture over a million points per second with accuracy measured in thousandths of an inch. 

Unlike consumer-grade scanners or tablet-based 3D capture tools, the FaroArm is metrology-grade. Its measurements are highly accurate, repeatable, and traceable to industry standards. This ensures that the data collected can be relied on for engineering decisions, quality control, and reverse engineering in applications where consumer-grade scanners simply cannot provide the same level of confidence. These metrology tools have become extremely useful in delivering engineered solutions that customers can trust.
