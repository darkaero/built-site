August 2025 Recap

Progress continued on the DarkAero 1 prototype in August, with efforts focused on molding the remaining multi-pi joint segments and beginning installation of these new structures in the center section of the wing. The improved flap actuation system mount design was completed, and more hardware, including a full airframe, was shipped out to fulfill customer contracts.
The upper and lower multi-pi joints with the center shear webs preassembled to check for fit and allgnment.

Center Wing Section 
The remaining multi-pi joint segments were molded in August, bringing the total to five: three lower segments and two upper segments. Maintaining dimensional accuracy of the parts was critical throughout the manufacturing process. Each multi-pi joint segment incorporates five slots that interface with corresponding honeycomb sandwich panels that form the wing center section shear webs. The fuel sump, avionics harnessing, seat bottom, and control system hardware are all tightly integrated with the center section of the wing. To limit the amount of additional neighboring system updates, the position of the center section shear webs has been maintained through the updated center wing design.  
The trimmed multi-pi joints prior to being installed in the wing.

After the parts were trimmed to final dimensions and inspected, efforts shifted to installing the multi-pi joints in the wing. Installation presented a number of challenges with alignment and fixturing.
The lower copilot side multi-pi joint being bonded and vacuum bagged into the wing.

Each of the three lower segments were individually bonded into the lower wing skin and vacuum bagged in place. Given the challenging geometry of the installation, each bonding operation was test fit multiple times before the actual bonding operation to identify any potential obstacles. Although the vacuum bag provides high clamping force through cure, it prohibits accurate visual alignment during final quality checks. Special alignment tools were developed to ensure all three segments would be positioned correctly on the lower wing skin in relation to each other while under the vacuum bag.

The two upper pi joint segments were also bonded into the wing individually, but these were mechanically clamped in place rather than vacuum bagged. Special clamping tools were made to maximize uniform clamping pressure across the large bond area. In addition to the clamping tools, part-specific alignment tools were also used for the upper pi joint segments to properly locate them relative to the wing and lower pi joint segments.

After installation of the lower and upper pi joint segments, assembly work proceeded to installation of honeycomb sandwich panel sections that bridge between the upper and lower pi joint segments. These panels were fabricated and CNC cut in-house. They include reinforced mounting hardware for the flap actuation mechanism as well as inspection ports to allow for visual inspection of the wing center section internals. 
The newly created multi-pi joints installed in the wing along with the pilot and copilot side shear webs bonded in place.

This tedious installation process is applicable only to the prototype aircraft, since these new structures are being installed in a prebuilt wing as a modification. This approach presented less work and a shorter timeline compared to building an entirely new wing. A simplified installation procedure and different center section structures will be used in new production DarkAero 1 wings.

The new flap actuation system mounting design will interface with the center wing section slightly differently than the original design. With the new design, aerodynamic loads within the system will be reacted both by the aft center shear web as well as the top surface of the center wing section. This new addition to the control system mount creates a larger moment arm to react loads, while minimizing additional space claim between aircraft occupants.

Testing for Scaled Production
The internal DarkAero composite database continues to grow with ongoing physical testing. Testing in composites is critical, as material and process variables can have a significant impact on structural performance. It is possible to simulate composite structures with FEA, but FEA has limitations and cannot be performed reliably in the absence of physical testing. Therefore, physical testing remains the gold standard for structural validation in composites. While significant testing was performed in the past to validate bonding practices, additional testing was recently conducted to confirm bonding processes in a scaled manufacturing environment. The latest testing ensures maximum bond strength is achieved through the current procedures performed by a larger team of manufacturing technicians.
