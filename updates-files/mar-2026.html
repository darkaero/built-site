March 2026 Recap

DarkAero 1 prototype engine mount and firewall structural load testing was completed in March with the remaining lateral load test cases passing the predetermined proof load values. After the test rig was taken down, focus shifted to the control system. Two large composite assemblies were shipped for two separate contract customers. Another session of the DarkAero Aerospace Composites Course was held, a new manufacturing engineer joined the team, and interviews continued.

Engine Mount and Firewall Load Testing
With the primarily vertical positive and negative g proof load test cases completed in February, the aircraft was reoriented to perform lateral load test cases in March. These tests still included portions of the four primary loads (thrust, torque, inertial, and gyroscopic). During aircraft operation, a portion of the four primary loads act in the lateral direction during specific scenarios such as uncoordinated turns, gusting conditions, or accelerations in yaw. With these flight scenarios occurring more infrequently and unintentionally than the conditions corresponding to forces acting in the vertical direction, the proof load characterization for lateral load tests is less defined. The more ambiguous real world flight conditions drove lateral load tests to summarize operating conditions into fewer test cases than the previous vertical load testing.

While these new conditions theoretically could have been performed while the aircraft was in a wings-level orientation, placing the fuselage on its side allows gravity to be leveraged and thus requires a simplified setup. Equipment from the vertical stabilizer proof load test rig was repurposed to quickly move into the remaining portions of this test campaign. Once the test rig was set up and verified to behave under load in a predictable manner, the aircraft and engine mount supported the applied loads without concern. 

Although it was lower total loading than the vertical test cases, lateral loading on the engine mount was actually the more interesting test to complete. Similar to other existing aircraft engine mounts, this load direction requires the engine block itself to react bending loads between the two primary engine mount structural members. This load transfer occurs through rubber engine mounts, sometimes referred to as spacers or vibration isolators, which introduce levels of uncertainty into analysis calculations. To further understand this load transfer and structural interaction prior to actually testing it on the fuselage, the engine mass simulator and engine mount were installed on a plywood test firewall that elastically distorted while under load and allowed visual inspection of the deformation mode of the engine mount. It was rewarding to see the prototype structures react the loads as intended both off and on the fuselage.

As time permits, the team will be working on releasing a YouTube video covering the load test setup and test campaign. The prototype engine mount used thus far for DarkAero 1 development is manufactured out of aluminum, and the design has now been structurally verified for flight testing. Prior to flight testing, a conversion of parts of the engine mount to steel alloys is being evaluated. This change would leverage steel’s superior fatigue characteristics for reacting engine torque pulses, as well as its higher melting point to ensure structural integrity in the event of a fire.

Structural Ground Testing Developement
With the prototype aircraft wings, wing mount lugs, vertical stabilizer, horizontal stabilizer, firewall, engine mount, and landing gear primary structures through proof load testing, the team is transitioning into flight readiness reviews. Flight test readiness reviews are used across the industry as an internally ‘formalized’ method of gaining program-level flight acceptance prior to transitioning into operational testing. Although there are still several ground test campaigns to complete prior to the first flight, multiple different aspects of safe and successful programs come from a well-prepared approach to final vehicle acceptance. Over the upcoming months, the team will be looking over all ground test work completed to date to ensure adequate testing has been completed with proper understanding to match the program’s risk stance. Through these reviews, any remaining ground test campaigns that need to be completed prior to first flight will be uncovered. The identified high-level ground tests and general milestones remaining are listed and actively tracked on the Milestones section of the aircraft website page.

Control System Work
The control system has been worked on periodically through the development of the DarkAero 1. As primary structure proof load tests required the airframe components to be installed in test rigs, flipped around in various orientations that block access to aircraft internals, and even dynamically dropped from a suspended attitude, control system work has been regularly interrupted. Although some piece part design updates could be completed outside the aircraft, a significant amount of unknowns become visible when the hardware is fully assembled, actuated, and load tested. With the aircraft primary structures through the majority of respective load testing, the aircraft is now more physically accessible for mechanical systems work to continue. 

Control system tasks moving forward will be dictated by what is determined to be necessary to reach an airworthy state for flight testing. In March, the team began reviewing previous control system load testing results and documentation as well as assessing what the next steps are. Different design updates have been modeled and manufactured since the last test campaign and will require a bit more work prior to physically testing the designs. Although still closely related to structural load testing, the mechanical control system work will be an exciting workflow shift for the team.

Courses
Session 27 of the DarkAero Aerospace Composites Course was held in March. Students attended to advance their composite knowledge and hands-on skill in applications ranging from UAVs to racecars. The next available session is scheduled for June 25-26. The course material has been continuously refined over the past four years, and covers both the theoretical and hands-on aspects of aerospace composite materials and manufacturing. Live demos of wet layup, vacuum bagging, infusion, and prepreg allow students to gain experience in a broad range of manufacturing processes so the right process can be selected for their application. Fiber reinforcements including carbon, glass, aramid, and boron fibers are discussed, as well as approachable methods for selecting fabric weave types, resin systems, core materials, adhesives, and equipment.
