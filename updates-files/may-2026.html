May 2026 Recap

The team focused on expanding capabilities on multiple fronts in May by hiring six new employees, adding equipment, and increasing shop space. Newly designed control system parts for the DarkAero 1 prototype were built up for ongoing ground test work. The team accelerated work on various contract manufacturing projects, including those that required a high production rate of precision parts and the coordination of multiple vehicle chassis assemblies. 
Manufacturing engineer, Andrew Ricks, examines the new steel version of the aileron torque tubes. 

Control System
Development on the DarkAero 1 control system has continued in between ground testing efforts over the past year, with attention in this area recently ramping up following completion of engine mount testing. In May, the team fabricated components for a new iteration of the aileron control system that transitions much of the structure over to steel to increase stiffness in the system.
Manufacturing technician, Tyler Knieriem, welding the newly-machined steel control system parts. 

Additional updates to the aileron controls have focused on increasing the mechanical leverage within the system to improve required control stick input forces. Fabrication and fit-up of the updated components are currently underway, with continued testing planned moving forward.

DarkAero Capabilities
Manufacturing capabilities continued to expand in May with plans finalizing to expand into additional shop space that will more than double the square footage dedicated to contract manufacturing. The expansion is intended to support continued team growth, increased manufacturing throughput, and additional equipment integration over the coming year.

The team also purchased a second Tormach 1500MX CNC mill, which is scheduled to be installed in June. The additional machine capacity will further expand in-house machining capability and reduce bottlenecks associated with fixture fabrication, tooling, and prototype hardware production.

When learning about composites, attention is often drawn to raw materials such as carbon fiber and epoxy resin. However, the molds and tooling required to produce high-quality composite components are just as critical and are often underappreciated. Continued interest from students led to the development of a dedicated course focused on this aspect of the process.

Previously referred to as the Mold Making Course, this offering has recently been expanded and renamed the DarkAero Aerospace Mold and Tooling Course. The updated curriculum now includes not only mold fabrication, but also assembly fixtures, drill jigs, trim tools, inspection gauges, and workholding strategies developed through the production of precision composite hardware for demanding aerospace applications. As projects scale from individual parts to full assemblies, these tooling considerations become increasingly important, and the course content has evolved to address that progression.
