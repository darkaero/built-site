Aileron Control System
Aileron control system work continued in June with full assembly installation in the aircraft and initial characterization of the redesigned system under simulated aerodynamic loads. The aileron control system is now manufactured with 4130 chromoly steel and the initial assembly had an acceptable amount of free play with little adjustment required. The test campaign began with characterizing the elasticity of the full system with one stick fixed and aerodynamic loads simultaneously applied to both ailerons. The elastic deformation of the system under applied load was then plotted against elasticity acceptance criteria and resulting predicted aircraft roll rates. The new aileron control system showed promising rigidity through these tests and provided enough confidence to move forward with further testing. 

After verifying adequate elasticity, the mechanical advantage, sometimes referred to as the gearing, of the roll control was measured to verify expected stick forces will be in line with predicted values. From a flight test perspective, it is important for the aircraft stick to not feel too light or too heavy in the pilot’s hand. The measured mechanical advantage approximately matched expected values across the entire aileron deflection range.

With the free play, elasticity, and mechanical advantage of the system characterized, the test campaign moved forward to proof load testing. While at 80% of the target proof load value, a steel bellcrank near the wing root plastically deformed. This test failure was traced back to an incorrectly performed finite element analysis that did not accurately replicate the real world load vector the component experiences during roll control actuation. The bellcrank component was redesigned, manufactured, bench tested, and installed prior to the end of the month.

DarkAero Capabilities
Manufacturing capability continued to expand in June with the installation of a second Tormach 1500MX CNC mill. In addition to increasing overall machining capacity, the new machine is equipped with a 4th axis, enabling more efficient machining of complex hardware that would otherwise require multiple setups. Since installation, the machines have already been operating simultaneously and producing hardware for both DarkAero 1 development and contract customer programs.

Build-out also began on the new manufacturing space announced in last month's update. Work in June focused on infrastructure improvements including additional lighting, expanded electrical service, and larger overhead doors to accommodate delivery and installation of larger CNC equipment in the future. Construction is expected to be completed late July.

To further support structural testing and validation efforts, a new data acquisition system was also added. The system is capable of simultaneously recording data from multiple sensor types, including strain gages, thermocouples, string potentiometers, accelerometers, and load cells. This expanded capability will support more sophisticated test programs by allowing a greater number of measurements to be collected, synchronized, and logged during a single test.

Process development also continued to manufacture composite parts with high-temperature capability. Certain resin systems require infusion at elevated temperature but provide significantly improved thermal performance compared to conventional infusion resins, with operational capability over 400°F. Over the past several months, manufacturing processes have been refined and validated, resulting in production flight hardware that has successfully entered flight testing on customer programs.

A lesson that has become increasingly apparent throughout development of the DarkAero 1 and other aircraft is that producing high-quality composite parts is only part of the challenge. As assemblies become larger and tolerances become tighter, success depends just as much on the tooling used to manufacture, locate, trim, inspect, and assemble those parts.

The DarkAero Aerospace Mold and Tooling Course was developed around this philosophy. In addition to mold fabrication, the course covers assembly fixtures, drill jigs, trim tools, inspection gauges, and workholding strategies developed through the production of precision composite aerospace hardware. These topics become critically important as projects transition from producing individual components to manufacturing complete assemblies in a repeatable manner.
